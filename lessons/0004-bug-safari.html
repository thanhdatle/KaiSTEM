KaiSTEM · Project 04 · ~1 week

Backyard Bug Safari

Kai becomes a tiny-creature detective. New superpower this project: classifying — looking closely and grouping living things by what's the same and what's different.

~30 min / day × 5 days· Interests: 🐛 animals · 👀 close looking · 🔢 counting· Difficulty: ★★★

The win this project earns

Kai already predicts and tests. Now he learns to look closely and sort living things into groups — by legs, wings, color, where they live. Classifying is how scientists make sense of a messy world, and it's deep early-math thinking dressed up as a bug hunt.1 Bonus win: gentleness and respect for small creatures.

The big idea: classifying

Classifying means grouping things by a rule — and the magic is that the same bugs can be grouped a new way when you pick a new rule (by color, then by legs, then by where they live). Re-sorting by a fresh rule is flexible thinking, and it's harder (and more fun) than the one-rule sorting from Project 1.

What you'll prepare

Prep time: ~10 min. Cost: a kids' magnifier is a few dollars; everything else is household. Best on: a warm, calm day — bugs hide in cold/wind. Have a rainy-day backup (see FAQ).

The gear

  • 1–2 magnifying glasses — the star tool. A clear bug-viewer jar with a magnifying lid is even better.
  • A clear catch container (jar or bug box) with air holes — for a quick, gentle, temporary look.
  • A soft tool to scoop — a spoon, a small paintbrush, or a leaf (never fingers on fragile bugs).
  • A white tray, plate, or sheet of paper — bugs show up beautifully against white.
  • Simple bug picture cards (print a few: ant, beetle, bee, butterfly, spider, worm, snail, ladybug, fly) — for matching and sorting indoors.
  • The Bug Journal + crayons.
  • Rainy-day kit: a tub of dried beans/rice + plastic toy bugs + tongs + the magnifier = an indoor "sensory safari."
  • Optional: sun hat, a field guide or a phone bug-ID app for you.

Safety & kindness check

Look, don't grab. Teach "we observe, then let them go home." Don't touch: bees/wasps, fuzzy/spiky caterpillars, spiders, biting ants, anything you don't recognize. Wash hands after. Watch for ticks in long grass; use sun protection. Release every bug where you found it. Never let a bug near his mouth.

Setup, before Day 1

  1. Print 6–9 bug picture cards (or sketch simple ones).
  2. Scout 3–4 "bug spots": under a rock/log, on flowers, in grass, near a damp wall or pot.
  3. Pack the safari kit in a basket so it's grab-and-go.
  4. Print the Bug Journal; set out crayons.

How each day fills ~30 minutes

Every day below is built in three layers — warm-up (~5) → main, several rounds (~15) → extend/stretch (~10) — plus a Stretch it further list for when Kai races ahead. Run extra rounds before adding new tasks; repetition is where it sticks. (More on this in the Playbook.)

Day by day, in detail

Day 1 · ★★★ · ≈30 min

First safari

Goal: find real bugs and observe them up close — build the "look REALLY close" habit.

Warm-up (~5 min)

Before going out: Where do you think bugs like to hide? Let's make a guess list! (under things, in flowers, in grass, near water). Hand him the magnifier and practise on your own hand/a leaf.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Visit your bug spots one at a time. At each: predict what you'll find, gently look, scoop one onto the white tray, and observe with the magnifier. Count its legs together. Move to the next spot and repeat — each habitat is a new round.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Pick a favorite bug; Kai draws it in the journal while looking through the magnifier. Tally how many bugs you found in total.

Ask

How many legs does it have? Let's count! Does it have wings?

What is it doing? Where do you think it was going?

Watch for

  • He looks closely instead of just grabbing — observation over impulse.
  • He counts legs and notices features (wings, spots, antennae).
  • If he wants to touch everything, model gentle scooping and "we look, then let it go."

Stretch it further

  • Bug bingo: who can spot something flying / crawling / with spots / very tiny?
  • Listen: close eyes — can you hear any bugs? Where's the sound?
  • Find the smallest bug and the biggest; compare.

Done when

He's observed a few bugs closely and drawn one. Release everyone.

Day 2 · ★★★ · ≈30 min

Sort & re-sort

Goal: classify — and then re-classify the same set by a new rule (the deep skill).

Warm-up (~5 min)

Lay out the bug picture cards (and/or toy bugs). Remember the bugs we found? Let's be sorting scientists today.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Sort by one rule first — say, color. Count each pile. Then mix them up and sort the SAME bugs a new way — by number of legs (6 vs 8 vs lots), then by wings / no wings, then by "found in the air vs on the ground." Each new rule is a fresh round.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Let Kai invent his OWN sorting rule and have you guess it. Make a simple "more / fewer" comparison: which group has the most?

Ask

Can you put the same kinds together? What makes them go together?

Now let's sort a different way — by how many legs. Where does the spider go?

Which group has the most? How can we check?

Watch for

  • He sorts consistently by the chosen rule.
  • He can switch rules and re-sort — flexible thinking (the big win).
  • Mixing up "insect vs spider" is normal — that's tomorrow's focus.

Stretch it further

  • Sort by two rules at once: "red AND flying."
  • Make a bug "family line-up" from smallest to biggest.

Done when

He's sorted the set at least two different ways and counted the groups.

Day 3 · ★★★ · ≈30 min

Insect or not?

Goal: a real classification rule — insects have 3 body parts and 6 legs.2 (Kid words: head, middle, bottom; six legs.)

Warm-up (~5 min)

Teach the secret rule with your fingers: "Insects have SIX legs and THREE parts. Spiders have EIGHT legs — they're not insects!" Count to six and eight together.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Go through bug cards/toys one at a time: count legs, count body parts, and decide — Insect? Or not an insect? Make two piles. Spider, worm, snail, centipede → "not insects." Ant, bee, beetle, butterfly → "insects!"

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Build a "perfect insect" from playdough (or pipe cleaners): three body parts, six legs, two antennae. Counting + representation + fine motor.

Ask

Let's count the legs — six? Then it's an insect! More than six?

Can you find the three body parts? Head… middle… bottom!

Watch for

  • He applies the rule ("8 legs — not an insect!").
  • Careful counting to 6 and 8.
  • He won't get every bug right — applying a rule at all is the achievement.

Stretch it further

  • Make a playdough spider too — count out 8 legs.
  • Hunt the house/garden for one real insect to test the rule on.

Done when

He's sorted insects vs not-insects and built a 6-legged creature.

Day 4 · ★★★ · ≈30 min

Habitat detective

Goal: connect each creature to where it lives and what it does.

Warm-up (~5 min)

Every bug has a home. Where do you think a worm lives? A bee? Guess together.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Go back outside and match bugs to homes: under wood/rocks (pill bugs, worms), on flowers (bees, butterflies), in webs (spiders), on leaves (caterpillars). Observe behavior up close: what's it doing — eating, carrying, hiding? Visit several micro-habitats as rounds.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Build a tiny temporary "bug hotel" in the catch jar (leaves, soil, a twig) and watch one bug explore for a few minutes — then release. Draw its home in the journal.

Ask

Why do you think it lives here? What does it need — food? a hiding spot?

What is it doing right now? What do you think it eats?

Watch for

  • He predicts where a bug will be based on its home.
  • He notices behavior, not just appearance.

Stretch it further

  • A mini fair test (callback to Project 2): do we find MORE bugs in the sunny spot or the shady spot? Count and compare.
  • Follow one ant — where does it go? Does it have friends?

Done when

He's matched a few bugs to homes and watched one behave.

Day 5 · ★★★ · Kai leads · ≈30 min

Kai's bug museum

Goal: ownership — Kai runs the whole safari and "curates" his findings.

Warm-up (~5 min)

You're the bug scientist today! What's your plan? Let him lead the route.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Kai hunts, observes, and gathers (gently, temporarily) his "museum." He sorts them his own way and gives them names. You only ask and scribe.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

He "presents" his museum to you (or a stuffed-animal audience): how many, how he grouped them, his favorite and why. Then a careful, ceremonial release: "Thank you, bugs!" Draw the museum in the journal.

Ask (only)

Tell me about your collection — how did you group them?

Which is your favorite? Why? How many did you find?

Watch for

  • He chooses his own grouping rule and explains it.
  • Pride, gentleness, and curiosity all at once.
  • If it becomes free play, great — he's bonding with the natural world.

Done when

He's presented and released his museum. Celebrate the curator!

What to observe (is it working?)

  • Green flags: he looks before grabbing; counts legs; sorts and re-sorts by different rules; uses words like insect, legs, wings, antennae, habitat; predicts where bugs hide; treats creatures gently.
  • Gently steer: rough handling → model gentleness and the "look, then release" rule; only wants to catch, not observe → slow down at the white tray with the magnifier.
  • Don't worry about: mixing up insect vs spider, "wrong" sorting, fear of certain bugs (respect it), or wanting to just dig in the dirt. Curiosity and kindness are the wins.

FAQ & troubleshooting

It's raining / cold / we have no yard.

Run an indoor "sensory safari": bury plastic toy bugs in a tub of dried beans or rice, and Kai digs them out with tongs, observes with the magnifier, and sorts them — same classification skills, no weather needed. A windowsill, a potted plant, or a walk to a park also work. Even hunting bugs in picture books counts.

He's scared of bugs.

Never push. Start with toy bugs and pictures, then observe a real one safely inside a clear jar from a distance. Let your own calm curiosity lead. Many kids warm up over a few days once they feel in control (he holds the jar, he decides how close). Respect a hard "no" on any specific bug.

He wants to keep / squish the bugs.

Set the rule up front: "We're scientists — we look, then let them go home to their families." Give catching a time limit and make the release a happy ritual ("Bye bug!"). If squishing is impulse-driven, switch to observing bugs in place (on the ground/leaf) without catching.

We can't find any bugs.

Look in the right places: under rocks, logs, and pots (damp spots), in flowers, in long grass, near outdoor lights at dusk, on a spider web. Warm mid-mornings are best. If truly none, switch to the indoor sensory safari and try outside another day — "where ARE they hiding?" is itself a good science question.

He keeps miscounting legs.

Totally normal at 3.5. Count together slowly, touching each leg (or pointing on the magnified view). The goal is the one-to-one counting habit, not a correct total. Celebrate the effort, model the right count, move on.

He insists a spider is an insect.

Don't make it a correction battle — make it a counting check: "Let's count… 1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8 — eight! Insects only have six, so spiders are something else: arachnids." The rule does the teaching. If it doesn't land today, it will with repetition.

How do I handle a bee or wasp showing up?

Calm and still: "We don't touch bees — we watch them from here." Don't swat (that provokes). Admire from a safe distance; bees on flowers are great to observe, never catch. Model that respect, not fear, keeps everyone safe.

Is it okay to keep a bug overnight?

Better not, for this age — most bugs need their habitat, food, and moisture, and a forgotten jar ends sadly (a hard lesson). Keep it to a few minutes of observation, then release. If he's keen on longer observation, a snail in a ventilated tub with a leaf for an hour is gentle and easy.

He finished everything in 10 minutes again.

Lean on the layers: run more rounds (more bug spots, more sorting rules), add a Stretch it further option, or hand him the lead early. And it's fine to stop short on a high note — then do a second short session later the same day. Quality and joy beat clock-watching.
