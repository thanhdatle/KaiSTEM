Kitchen Color Lab

Mixing, magic colors, and the big fizz. New superpower this project: predicting a change — not just "where does it go?" but "what will it turn into?"

~30 min × 3 days· Interests: 💧 mixing · 🔢 counting · 🎨 color· Difficulty: ★★★ → ★★★★

The win this project earns

So far Kai has predicted positions (sink/float) and growth (will it grow). Now he predicts transformations: two colors become a third, a powder and a liquid erupt into fizz. This is the leap to "I can make something new happen" — and it's pure delight, which is the whole point.

The method (now with bigger surprises)

Same loop. The "Predict" step gets richer: not just yes/no, but "what will it become?" Scripts & question bank: Parent Playbook.

  • 👀Notice
    2 colors, a powder
  • 💭Wonder
    "what if they mix?"
  • 🤔Predict
    "it'll turn into…"
  • 🧪Test
    drip & mix!
  • 💬Talk
    "what happened?!"

What you'll prepare

Prep time: ~10 min. Cost: a box of baking soda + a bottle of vinegar + food coloring — a few dollars, and the leftovers clean your kitchen. Mess level: high and joyful — set up where spills don't matter.

The gear

  • A muffin tin or ice-cube tray — the "lab bench" with little wells for colors.
  • Food coloring or liquid watercolors: red, yellow, blue (the primaries — these make every other color).
  • 2–3 eyedroppers or pipettes (or a turkey baster, or small spoons). Droppers are the star tool — they build serious fine-motor strength and let Kai add liquid drop by drop.1
  • Baking soda — a whole box (for Days 2–3).
  • White vinegar — a bottle.
  • A shallow tray or rimmed baking dish to contain the fizz.
  • Small cups for mixing colored vinegar; a smock/old shirt; paper towels.
  • The Lab Journal + crayons (so he can color his predictions).

Safety check

Everything here is non-toxic and kitchen-safe — but no taste-testing (vinegar is unpleasant and food coloring stains tongues and clothes). Keep it away from eyes. Stay alongside the whole time. Protect the table; food coloring stains grout, wood, and fabric.

Setup, before you start each day

  1. Day 1: fill three wells with water, one tinted red, one yellow, one blue. Leave empty wells for mixing. Lay out droppers.
  2. Day 2: spread a layer of baking soda across the tray. Mix vinegar with a few drops of color in small cups.
  3. Day 3: put everything out as an open "lab" — baking soda, colored vinegars, droppers, spoons, empty cups — and let Kai run it.

Day by day, in detail

Day 1 · ★★★

Magic color mixing

Goal: predict a transformation — a brand-new color appearing — and build dropper control.

Do

  1. Show the three primary colors in their wells. Name them together.
  2. Before mixing, get a guess: red + yellow = ? Let him dropper them into an empty well.
  3. Reveal: orange! Repeat for yellow+blue (green) and red+blue (purple).
  4. Count the drops aloud as he squeezes ("one… two… three drops of red!").

Ask

What do you think red and yellow will make? Let's guess, then mix!

Whoa — what color is that? Did you guess it?

What should we mix next? What will THAT make?

Watch for

  • He predicts a color before mixing (even a wild guess).
  • Genuine wonder when the new color appears.
  • Improving dropper control — squeezing, aiming, counting drops.
  • Mixing everything into one muddy brown is a classic, fun discovery — not a mistake.

Done when

He's made at least one new color from a prediction. Save the rainbow to admire.

Day 2 · ★★★★

The big fizz

Goal: predict a dramatic reaction — his first real chemistry.

Do

Spread baking soda in the tray. Hold up a cup of colored vinegar and build suspense before the first drip. Then let him dropper it on — FIZZ! Repeat with each color, making colorful fizzing puddles. Let him go big; this is the wow day.

Ask

What do you think happens when this drips on the powder? Let's guess first!

Look at the bubbles! That's gas — like a fizzy drink!2

What does it sound like? What happens if we add MORE?

Watch for

  • He predicts "something will happen" and is thrilled when it does.
  • He notices the bubbles, the sound, the fizz dying down.
  • He wants to do it again — repetition cements the cause→effect link.
  • He won't understand acid + base; "it makes gas/bubbles" is plenty.

Done when

He's made the fizz happen several times and described what he saw.

Day 3 · ★★★★ · Kai leads

Kai's potion lab

Goal: open-ended invention + reusing the "change one thing" fair test from Project 2.

Do

Everything's out; Kai is the chief scientist. He invents "potions," predicting each before he mixes. Then set the challenge: how do we make the BIGGEST fizz? Change just one thing — more baking soda? more vinegar? — and compare. You only ask and scribe.

Ask

You're in charge — what's your potion? What do you think it'll do?

How could we make a bigger fizz? Let's change just ONE thing and see.

Which one fizzed more? What did we change?

Watch for

  • He predicts before each mix without being told.
  • He grasps "change one thing" from Project 2 in a brand-new setting (this is the deep win).
  • He invents his own variations and questions.
  • Total chaos-potion-making is fine — he's experimenting. Steer toward one fair test if he's up for it, but don't force it.

Done when

He's run his own potions and tried at least one "biggest fizz" comparison. Celebrate the chief scientist!

Interleaving — why the Day 3 callback matters

Bringing back the "fair test" from Project 2 inside a totally different activity is deliberate. Mixing old skills into new contexts (interleaving) is one of the most reliable ways to make learning stick long-term, not just feel easy in the moment.

What to observe (is it working?)

  • Green flags: he predicts before mixing/dripping; he's delighted by the transformations; he wants to repeat and vary things; he reuses "change one thing"; he uses words like mix, color names, fizz, bubbles, gas.
  • Gently steer: if he rushes straight to dumping everything, slow down for one predicted mix, then let him free-play; if the fizz overwhelms him, do smaller amounts.
  • Don't worry about: muddy colors, not understanding the chemistry, wild "potions," or skipping the fair test if he's not in the mood. Joy and prediction are the goals.

FAQ & troubleshooting

He just dumps everything together instead of predicting.

Expected — the materials are irresistible. Get one predicted mix in first ("just guess this ONE, then you can play"), then let him free-mix. The prediction habit and the free play can take turns; you don't need every drop to be an experiment.

All the colors turned brown.

That's the universal toddler discovery, and it's genuinely science: too many colors mix to brown. Name it cheerfully ("we made mud!"), then reset with fresh wells and try just two colors at a time so the new color stays clear.

The fizz isn't impressive.

Boost it: use fresh baking soda (old loses fizz), pile the powder thicker, and use straight vinegar (not too watered down). Warm vinegar fizzes a bit more. A squirt of dish soap makes foamier, longer-lasting bubbles — a fun Day 3 variable.

Is baking soda and vinegar really safe for a 3.5-year-old?

Yes — it's a non-toxic, kitchen-safe acid-base reaction; the only product is carbon dioxide gas (the fizz), plus water and salt.2 Just no eating, keep it out of eyes, and supervise. The smell of vinegar is the most likely complaint.

He wants to taste it / put the dropper in his mouth.

Head it off up front: "This is for testing, not tasting — scientists never taste their experiments." Vinegar tastes bad enough that one accidental sip usually ends the urge, but stay close. If mouthing persists, switch him to spoons and you handle the droppers.

How do I protect my kitchen from stains?

Food coloring stains grout, wood, and fabric. Work on a wipeable tray or a silicone mat, dress him in old clothes, and wipe spills fast. Doing it outside or in the bathtub removes all the worry. Liquid watercolors stain less than gel food coloring.

He's scared of the fizz.

Start tiny — a single drop on a pinch of baking soda, at a distance, with you doing it first. Let him watch until curiosity beats caution. Never push. Some kids warm up by Day 2 or want to control it themselves with the dropper.

He won't do the "fair test" on Day 3 — just wants to play.

Fine. The fair test is a bonus, not a requirement. If the open play is rich and he's predicting, you've already won. Slip in one comparison if a natural moment appears ("ooh, which one fizzed more?"), but don't turn play into a lesson he resists.

Can we make it bigger — a "volcano"?

Absolutely, as a finale: pack baking soda in a small cup or bottle, add a squirt of dish soap and colored vinegar for an erupting foam "volcano." Do it in the tray or outside. Predict first: "How high will it go?" Ask me for a full volcano variant if he's hooked.
