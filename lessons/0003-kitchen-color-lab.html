KaiSTEM · Project 03 · 3 days

Kitchen Color Lab

Mixing, magic colors, and the big fizz. New superpower this project: predicting a change — not just "where does it go?" but "what will it turn into?"

~30 min × 3 days· Interests: 💧 mixing · 🔢 counting · 🎨 color· Difficulty: ★★★ → ★★★★

The win this project earns

So far Kai has predicted positions (sink/float) and growth (will it grow). Now he predicts transformations: two yellows-and-blues become green, a powder and a liquid erupt into fizz. This is the leap to "I can make something new happen" — and it's pure delight, which is the whole point.

The method (now with bigger surprises)

Same loop. The "Predict" step gets richer: not just yes/no, but "what will it become?" Scripts & question bank: Parent Playbook.

  • 👀Notice
    2 colors, a powder
  • 💭Wonder
    "what if they mix?"
  • 🤔Predict
    "it'll turn into…"
  • 🧪Test
    drip & mix!
  • 💬Talk
    "what happened?!"

Prep once (the night before Day 1)

A muffin tin or ice-cube tray is your lab bench. Eyedroppers/pipettes are the star tool — they build serious fine-motor control and let Kai add liquid drop by drop.1

  • A muffin tin or ice-cube tray (the "lab bench")
  • Food coloring or liquid watercolors: red, yellow, blue
  • 2–3 eyedroppers, pipettes, or a turkey baster (or small spoons)
  • Baking soda (a whole box) + white vinegar (for Days 2–3)
  • A shallow tray or baking dish with a rim (for the fizz mess)
  • Small cups for mixing colored vinegar; a smock/old shirt; a wipe-down surface
  • The Lab Journal below + crayons

⚠️ All non-toxic, but no taste-testing (vinegar smell + food coloring stains). Adult alongside the whole time.

The three-day arc

Day 1

Magic color mixing

★★★ · predicting a new color

Fill tray wells with red, yellow, blue water. What do you think red and yellow make? Let's guess, then mix! Kai droppers them together → orange! Then yellow+blue → green, red+blue → purple. Count the drops as you go.

You're building: predicting a transformation + dropper fine-motor + counting.

Day 2

The big fizz

★★★★ · a real reaction

Spread baking soda in the tray. Mix vinegar with a color in a cup. What do you think happens when this drips on the powder? Drip → FIZZ! Talk: It's making bubbles — that's gas, like a fizzy drink!2 Repeat with each color.

You're building: predicting a dramatic change; first taste of chemistry.

Day 3

Kai's potion lab

★★★★ · Kai leads + a fair test

Lay out everything; Kai invents "potions," predicting each one before he mixes. Then a challenge that calls back to Project 2: How can we make the BIGGEST fizz? Let's change just one thing — more baking soda? more vinegar? Try it as a fair test.

You're building: open-ended invention + re-using the "change one thing" idea.

Interleaving — why the Day 3 callback matters

Bringing back the "fair test" from Project 2 inside a totally different activity is deliberate. Mixing old skills into new contexts (interleaving) is one of the most reliable ways to make learning stick long-term, not just feel easy in the moment.
