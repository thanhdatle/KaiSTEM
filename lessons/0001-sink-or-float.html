KaiSTEM · Project 01 · Week 1

Sink or Float?

Five days at the water tub. Kai learns the one move at the heart of all science: guess first, then find out. Your job is to ask, wait, and splash along.

~30 min / day × 5 days· Interests: 💧 water · 🐛 nature · 🔢 counting· Difficulty: ★☆☆ (start here)

The win this project earns

By Friday, Kai will spontaneously guess before trying things — at the tub, at bath time, at dinner. That habit ("predict, then test") is the seed of every science to come. Facts about why things float are a bonus; the habit is the goal.

The method (same every project)

Run this loop on each object. Don't lecture — narrate and ask. Full scripts and the question bank are in the Parent Playbook.

  • 👀Notice
    hold it, feel it
  • 💭Wonder
    "I wonder…"
  • 🤔Predict
    "will it stay up?"
  • 🧪Test
    plop!
  • 💬Talk
    "what happened?"

What you'll prepare

Prep time: ~10 minutes the night before. Cost: basically free — it's all stuff around the house. One tub + one tray of objects covers all five days; you only add foil (Day 3) and a nature basket (Day 4).

The gear

  • A water container: a big plastic tub, a deep mixing bowl, a dishpan, or just the bathtub. Deeper is better — things need room to actually sink.
  • 2 towels — one under the tub for spills, one for drying hands.
  • An old shirt or smock, sleeves rolled. Assume he gets wet to the elbows.
  • 2 small bowls or trays for sorting on Day 2, with a hand-drawn ⬆ FLOAT card and ⬇ SINK card.
  • 1 sheet of aluminum foil + a few coins or a small ball of playdough (Day 3).
  • The printed journal page + crayons or dot stickers.
  • Optional but lovely: a slotted spoon, ladle, or small net for fishing things out (great fine-motor practice).

Choosing the test objects (this is the important part)

Pick 8–10 small, safe objects that will split your results — some that clearly float, some that clearly sink, and 2–3 genuine surprises. Surprises are where the learning lives, so seed a few on purpose.

Safety check before you start

Stay within arm's reach of the water the entire time — drowning happens fast and quietly in even a few inches. Skip anything small enough to choke on if it went in his mouth, and anything sharp or that ruins in water (electronics, paper books).

Setup, the night before

  1. Gather the 8–10 objects into the tray. Quietly predict each one yourself so you're not surprised mid-activity.
  2. Make the ⬆ FLOAT and ⬇ SINK cards and the two sorting bowls; set them aside for Day 2.
  3. Print one journal page per day (or rule a quick grid on paper).
  4. Pick your spot — somewhere splashes don't matter (kitchen floor, bathroom, patio).

The arc at a glance

Same loop every day, one notch harder. Stop any day while it's still fun — you can stretch a day across two if attention runs short.

Day by day, in detail

Day 1 · ★☆☆

Guess & splash

Goal: install the reflex to guess before acting. Nothing more. Keep it light and fast.

Set up (2 min)

Fill the tub, lay the towel, roll his sleeves. Put the tray of objects beside it. Don't explain the science — just say Let's play a guessing game with water!

Do

Hand him one object at a time. Before it touches the water, get a guess. Then let him drop it. Big, genuine reactions to surprises. Do 5–6 objects, then stop.

Ask

Hold it — do you think this will stay on top, or go down? What's your guess?

Why do you think so? (Any answer is great — "because it's heavy," "because I like it.")

Ready? You drop it!

Watch for

  • He pauses to guess before dropping (even once) — that's the whole win.
  • Surprise on his face when a guess is "wrong" — that's curiosity igniting.
  • If he just wants to dump everything in at once, let him for a minute, then bring back one-at-a-time with "let's guess this one."

Done when

He's guessed before at least a couple of drops, or after ~10–15 min. End on a high note.

Day 2 · ★★☆

Sort & count

Goal: add classifying (group by one rule) and counting — early math that strongly predicts later school success.

Set up (3 min)

Same tub. Add the two bowls with ⬆ FLOAT and ⬇ SINK cards.

Do

Test each object again (still predict first!). After each, fish it out and put it in the matching bowl. At the end, count both piles together, touching each item as you count.

Ask

This one floated — which bowl does it go in?

Let's count the floaters! One… two… three… How many?

Which bowl has more? How can we tell?

Watch for

  • He sorts correctly based on what he saw (evidence → category).
  • He counts along, even if numbers get jumbled — point-and-count is the skill.
  • Miscounting ("one, two, five!") is totally normal at 3.5 — just model the right count cheerfully.

Done when

Both bowls are sorted and you've counted each at least once.

Day 3 · ★★☆

Make it change

Goal: the first taste of engineering — "I can change what happens."

Set up (2 min)

Have the foil sheet and a few coins (or a playdough ball) ready. Confirm first that the coins sink: drop one in together.

Do

Pose the challenge, then build a foil boat together and load the coins. It floats! Then let him crush the boat into a tight ball and watch it sink again. Repeat — kids love the repetition, and repetition is where it sticks.

Ask

The coins sank. I wonder… could we make them float? What if we built a boat?

What happened to the boat when you squished it? Why do you think it sank that time?

Watch for

  • Delight/surprise that the same coins can float or sink depending on the boat.
  • He starts changing things himself ("what if I add more coins?").
  • He won't grasp why (surface area / displacement) — that's fine. The takeaway is "I changed the result," not the physics.

Done when

He's made the boat float and seen the crushed ball sink at least once.

Day 4 · ★★★

Nature hunt

Goal: transfer the idea to messy, real materials — and hook his nature interest.

Set up (10 min, outdoors)

Go outside first. Collect a basket together: rocks, leaves, sticks, an acorn, a flower, bark, a pinecone. Let him choose. Bring it all to the tub.

Do

Predict each natural object before testing. Expect surprises (a fat stick floats; a small dense rock sinks instantly). Sort and count again if he's into it.

Ask

Where did you find this? What does it feel like — heavy or light?

Why do you think the leaf floats but the rock sinks?

I wonder if all sticks float, or just this one?

Watch for

  • He carries over the "guess first" habit without you prompting.
  • He notices patterns ("the wood ones float!").
  • If outside becomes the whole adventure and the tub gets forgotten — that's fine, nature observation IS the science. Test indoors later.

Done when

He's tested a handful of natural finds and made at least one comparison.

Day 5 · ★★★ · Kai leads

Kai's experiment

Goal: ownership — the leap from "doing an activity" to "having an idea and testing it."

Set up (5 min)

Empty tub, ready. No tray this time.

Do

Send him on a hunt: You're the scientist today — go find 5 things you want to test! You only veto for safety. He predicts, tests, sorts, and counts. You ask questions and scribe — you do not drive.

Ask (only)

What should we test first? What's your guess for this one?

Ooh, that surprised you! What happened?

What do you want to try next?

Watch for

  • He chooses things because he's curious how they'll behave.
  • He guesses unprompted — the habit has landed.
  • If his "experiment" is mostly joyful splashing, that's a win too. He's associating science with fun.

Done when

He's run his own 5 tests. Celebrate: You did real science today!

The full question bank (for this project)

Pick one or two — never interrogate. Ask, then wait five seconds before rescuing the silence. (General scripts live in the Playbook.)

MomentSay this
Before a drop"Will it stay up or go down? What's your guess?"
To stretch a guess"Why do you think so?"
At a surprise"Whoa! Did you think that would happen?"
To compare"How is this one different from that one?"
To find a pattern"Do all the heavy ones sink? Let's check!"
To hand over control"What should we try next? You decide."
To wonder aloud (you)"I wonder why the big orange floats but the tiny grape sinks…"
To close"What was the most surprising thing today?"

What to observe (is it working?)

You're not grading Kai. You're watching for signs the curiosity habit is forming. Process beats product every time at this age.

  • Green flags: he pauses to guess; he reacts to surprises; he wants to test "just one more"; he starts asking his own "what if?"; he uses words like float, sink, guess.
  • Gently redirect: if he only dumps everything in, slow the pace to one object; if he loses interest, shorten the session, don't push.
  • Don't worry about: wrong guesses (the point!), miscounting, not knowing why things float, going off-script, or wanting to repeat the same thing ten times (repetition = consolidation).

FAQ & troubleshooting

He won't make a guess — he just wants to drop things.

Totally normal. Don't force it. Make the guess a tiny game instead: you guess out loud first ("I think… SINK!"), then ask "what do YOU think?" Or give a two-choice gesture — thumbs up for float, down for sink. If he still won't, just let him explore; the guessing often shows up on Day 2 or 3 once the pattern feels safe.

He gives "wrong" answers. Do I correct him?

Never correct a prediction — test it. "Let's find out!" is the magic phrase. Being wrong and seeing it is the single most valuable moment in the whole project. Correcting him teaches him to fear guessing; testing teaches him that guesses are just the start of finding out.

He loses interest after 5 minutes.

Five focused minutes at 3.5 is a success, not a failure. "30 minutes" includes setup and mess — real focused attention is often 10–15 min. Stop the moment interest dips, while it's still fun, so he's eager to come back. You can split one "day" across two short sessions.

He just wants to splash and make a mess.

Let him — for a bit. Splashing is sensory play and it's bonding. Then re-enter gently: "Hey, I found something tricky — do you think THIS floats?" Ride the energy instead of fighting it. A joyful, messy session still builds his "science is fun" identity, which is the real mission.

The "surprise" objects didn't surprise him.

No problem — he may not have strong expectations yet. The surprises matter more later. You can manufacture a tiny one: "Watch this!" then float something heavy-looking (a big plastic toy) or sink something small (a coin). Your own delight is contagious.

Should I explain WHY things float?

No. Buoyancy/density is abstract and won't land at 3.5 — it just eats the working memory he needs for the fun part. If he asks "why?", a great answer is "What a good question — I wonder too! What do YOU think?" Keep it about noticing and guessing, not mechanisms. (The word "buoyancy" lives in the glossary for you, not him.)

How do I keep the mess manageable?

Do it in the bathtub, on a tiled floor, or outside. Towel under the tub, old clothes, half-fill the tub (less to splash out). Make cleanup part of the ritual: "Scientists tidy their lab — can you dry the floaters?" Drying objects is itself fine-motor practice.

A sibling wants to join.

Great — science is social. Give each child their own job (one drops, one sorts, one counts) and take turns guessing. For mixed ages, let the older one predict and explain their reasoning while the younger just drops and reacts.

Is 3.5 too young for this?

Not at all. Preschoolers already reason about cause and effect and make predictions — this project just gives that instinct a structure. If a step feels too hard, drop to the previous day's level. If he breezes through, jump ahead to Project 2.

Can we do this without screens, or should I show a video first?

This is best fully hands-on — no screen needed. If you like, watch the primary-source video below yourself beforehand to calibrate your pacing. Kai learns this one by doing, not watching.
