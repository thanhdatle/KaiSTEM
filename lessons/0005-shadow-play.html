Shadow Play

Catch a shadow, change it, measure it, and discover it moves all by itself. New superpower this project: measuring & comparing, and spotting a pattern over a day.

~30 min / day × 5 days· Interests: ☀️ outdoors · 🔢 measuring · ✨ wonder· Difficulty: ★★★ → ★★★★

The win this project earns

Kai has counted and sorted; now he measures and compares — longer, shorter, bigger, smaller — and uncovers a real pattern: shadows change through the day because the sun moves. It's his first taste of physics, and it's genuinely magical.1

The big idea: a shadow needs a light + something to block it

A shadow appears when an object blocks a light source. Three-year-olds can absolutely grasp "the sun (or torch) makes it, and my body blocks it."1 Move the light or the object, and the shadow changes — that's the lever Kai gets to pull.

What you'll prepare

Prep time: ~5 min. Cost: a flashlight + sidewalk chalk — a couple of dollars. Two settings: indoor torch play (any time, any weather) and outdoor sun-shadow tracing (needs a sunny day). Plan the outdoor days around sunshine.

The gear

  • A flashlight / torch (or a phone torch, or a bright lamp) — your indoor "sun."
  • A blank wall or a hung white sheet — the shadow screen.
  • A handful of objects with interesting outlines: a toy figure, a fork, a cup, a leaf, a toy dinosaur, a clear plastic lid (the "does light go through?" surprise).
  • Sidewalk chalk — for tracing shadows outside.
  • Paper + crayons/pencil — for tracing small shadows indoors.
  • A "ruler": a tape measure, OR footsteps / hand-spans / a stack of blocks to measure shadow length.
  • The Shadow Journal.
  • Optional: a sunny window, white paper for shadow-tracing toys, a sticky note to mark "stand here."

Safety check

Never look directly at the sun — look at the shadows on the ground instead. Don't shine the torch in anyone's eyes. Use sun protection outdoors. Choose a safe, flat, traffic-free spot (driveway, patio, yard) for chalk tracing.

Setup, before you start

  1. Indoor days: pick a room you can darken; clear a wall; set out the torch + objects.
  2. Outdoor days: check the forecast for sun; pick a flat chalk-friendly spot; grab chalk + the journal.
  3. Decide your "measuring stick" (footsteps and blocks are most fun for him).
  4. Print the Shadow Journal.

How each day fills ~30 minutes

Each day is built in three layers — warm-up (~5) → main, several rounds (~15) → extend/stretch (~10) — plus Stretch it further options for a fast finisher. Run more rounds before adding tasks; repetition cements it. (See the Playbook.)

Day by day, in detail

Day 1 · ★★★ · ≈30 min

Make a shadow

Goal: discover that a shadow needs a light + something to block it — and that moving the light changes the size.

Warm-up (~5 min)

Darken the room. Make hand shadows on the wall with the torch — a bird, a dog. Where do shadows come from? What's making them?

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Shine the torch on object after object; each is a round. Predict the shadow first, then reveal. Then the lever: move the torch close then far — Will the shadow get bigger or smaller? Test it. Let Kai hold the torch and control it.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Shadow puppet show, or a "guess the object from its shadow" game. Try the clear plastic lid — does it make a shadow? (Light passes through!)

Ask

What happens to the shadow when I bring the light closer? Let's guess, then try!

What do we need to make a shadow? (a light AND something to block it)

Watch for

  • He says a light + an object make a shadow.
  • He predicts bigger/smaller and tests by moving the torch.
  • He won't explain why closer = bigger — discovering the effect is enough.

Stretch it further

  • Sort objects: which made a shadow, which let light through?
  • Make the SAME object's shadow tiny, then giant.

Done when

He's made shadows change size on purpose.

Day 2 · ★★★ · ≈30 min

Shape detective

Goal: predict a shadow's shape, and notice rotating an object changes it.

Warm-up (~5 min)

Hold an object behind your back: Guess which toy this is just from its shadow! Reveal and cheer.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

For each object: predict the shadow shape, then cast it. Then rotate the object — the shadow changes! (A cup from the side vs the top; a fork turned flat.) Many quick rounds keep it lively.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Trace small shadows on paper with the torch held still — Kai draws around the outline. Make a little gallery of traced shadows.

Ask

What shape will this shadow be? Round? Pointy?

Watch — I'm turning it. What happened to the shadow?

Watch for

  • He predicts shapes and is delighted by rotation surprises.
  • He matches objects to shadows correctly.

Stretch it further

  • Make a shadow that looks like a totally different thing by rotating it.
  • Sort traced shadows biggest → smallest.

Done when

He's predicted/matched several shapes and traced a few.

Day 3 · ★★★★ · outdoors · ≈30 min

Chalk shadow trace

Goal: measuring — capture a real sun-shadow and put a number on it.

Warm-up (~5 min)

Go out in the morning sun. Can you find your shadow? Can you make it wave? Can you step on mine? Shadow tag is a perfect hook.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Kai stands on a marked spot; trace his shadow with chalk. Then measure it — How many footsteps long is your shadow? Count together. Trace and measure a few things (a chair, a toy, a pole) as rounds.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Decorate the traced shadow into a drawing (give it a face!). Record the length in the journal. Mark his "stand here" spot clearly — you'll need it tomorrow / later today.

Ask

Which way does your shadow point? Is it longer or shorter than you?

How many footsteps long is it? Let's measure!

Watch for

  • He measures by counting steps/blocks (one-to-one).
  • He compares — "longer than me!"
  • Measuring won't be precise; the comparing language is the goal.

Stretch it further

  • Measure with different units — footsteps AND blocks. Which gives a bigger number?
  • Trace a friend/sibling too and compare lengths.

Done when

His shadow is traced, measured, and the spot is marked.

Day 4 · ★★★★ · outdoors · ≈30 min

The sun moved!

Goal: the pattern-over-time payoff — same spot, different shadow.

Warm-up (~5 min)

Return to the marked spot a few hours later (or midday vs late afternoon). Do you think your shadow will be the same as this morning, or different? Predict first.

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Kai stands on the SAME spot; trace again in a new chalk color over the old outline. The reveal: it points a different way and is a different length! Measure the new one and compare to the morning's number. Re-trace a couple of objects too.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

Talk about the secret: the sun moved across the sky, so the shadow moved. (Don't over-explain — the visual does the work.) Record both lengths in the journal side by side.

Ask

Whoa — it's different! What changed? Where's the sun now vs this morning?

Is this shadow longer or shorter than the morning one? By how much?

Watch for

  • He notices the shadow moved and connects it to the sun.
  • He compares the two measurements (longer/shorter).
  • "The sun moves" is the takeaway; orbital mechanics can wait ~10 years.

Stretch it further

  • A third trace near sunset — make a "shadow clock" of three colored outlines.
  • Predict tomorrow morning's shadow before you check.

Done when

He's seen and measured the change, and linked it to the sun moving.

Day 5 · ★★★★ · Kai leads · ≈30 min

Kai's shadow show

Goal: ownership + creativity — Kai directs the shadows.

Warm-up (~5 min)

You're the shadow boss today! What should we make?

Main — several rounds (~15 min)

Kai runs a shadow puppet show against the wall/sheet (hands, toys, paper cut-outs on sticks), inventing a little story. He controls the torch to make characters grow and shrink.

Extend / stretch (~10 min)

A "shadow challenge" he sets: find/make the biggest shadow, the smallest, the funniest; measure the biggest. Or take it outside for a sun-shadow show. Draw a scene in the journal.

Ask (only)

How did you make it so big? What's your story about?

Which shadow is the biggest? How can we tell?

Watch for

  • He controls size/shape on purpose and narrates.
  • He reuses "closer = bigger" and measuring without prompting.
  • Pure imaginative play is a win — wonder is the mission.

Done when

He's directed his own shadow show. Take a bow!

What to observe (is it working?)

  • Green flags: he says a light + object make a shadow; predicts bigger/smaller; measures by counting; uses longer/shorter/bigger/smaller; notices the shadow moved with the sun; controls shadows on purpose.
  • Gently steer: if measuring frustrates him, drop to simple "longer/shorter" comparisons; if indoor torch play overwhelms, do it in dimmer (not pitch-black) light.
  • Don't worry about: imprecise measuring, not understanding why the sun moves, or veering into pure puppet play. Comparing language and wonder are the wins.

FAQ & troubleshooting

It's cloudy — no sun for the outdoor days.

Run the indoor torch days (1, 2, 5) any time, and save the chalk-tracing days (3, 4) for sunshine. A bright lamp or even a strong window beam can stand in for a sun shadow on paper if you're stuck. The "it changed over time" idea also works indoors by moving the lamp to a new spot between traces.

He's afraid of the dark for the torch play.

You don't need pitch black — just dim. Leave a soft light on, sit close, and let him hold the torch (control reduces fear). Or do it all outdoors in daylight with sun shadows instead, skipping the dark room entirely.

His "measuring" is all over the place.

Perfect for 3.5 — precision isn't the point. Use big, fun units (his own footsteps, jumps, or blocks) and count together, touching each step. If numbers are a struggle, just use "longer / shorter / about the same." The comparison is the skill.

He doesn't get why the shadow moved.

He doesn't need to — seeing it move is the whole lesson. Keep it concrete: "This morning the sun was over there; now it's over here, so the shadow swung around." Point at the sun's position (never look at it directly). The "aha" is "shadows change through the day," not astronomy.

The chalk traces washed away / we can't reuse the spot.

Mark the standing spot with something permanent-ish (a rock, a taped X, a scratch in the dirt) so you can return precisely. If rain's coming, do both traces the same day (morning and afternoon). On paper indoors, just tape the sheet down so the object/lamp positions stay fixed.

He wants to do shadow puppets the whole time.

Let him — it's joyful and still science (he's controlling a light source and an object). Weave in one prediction or measurement ("how big can you make the dragon?") and call it a win. Save the structured tracing for a fresh day.

He finished in 10 minutes again.

Add rounds (more objects, more traces), pull a Stretch it further option, or hand him the torch/chalk to lead. A "shadow clock" (trace at three times of day) naturally stretches the project across a whole day in short bursts.

Is staring toward the sun to find shadows dangerous?

Looking at the sun is — never do that. But looking at the shadows on the ground is completely safe and where all the action is anyway. Keep eyes down on the shadows, hats on, and you're good.
