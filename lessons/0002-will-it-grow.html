Will It Grow?

Kai plants seeds and watches them wake up. New superpower this project: a fair test — change just one thing, and watch what happens over many days.

Day 1: ~30 min · then ~10 min/day· Interests: 🐛 nature · 💧 water · 🔢 counting· Difficulty: ★★☆

The win this project earns

In Project 1, Kai learned guess then test in one splash. Here he learns that some answers take time and watching — and that scientists find things out by changing just one thing (a "fair test"). By the end he'll point at the dark cup vs the sunny cup and tell you why they're different.

The big idea: a fair test

We make the two cups exactly the same — same seeds, same water, same paper towel — and change only one thing: light. One cup by the window, one in a dark cupboard. That's the secret: if you change one thing only, you know what made the difference.1 (We don't say "variable" — we say "let's change just one thing.")

What you'll prepare

Prep time: ~15 min to gather; ~20 min hands-on with Kai on Day 1. Cost: a bag of dried beans is pennies; everything else is household. Heads-up: start this when you'll be home for two weeks of daily check-ins.

The gear

  • 2 clear cups or jars, identical — clear so Kai can watch roots grow against the glass. Glass jars or clear plastic cups both work.
  • 4 dried beans (lima, kidney, or pinto) — 2 per cup. Big beans sprout fast and are easy for little hands to place. Soak them in water overnight first to speed germination.
  • 2 paper towels — crumpled to line each cup, so beans press against the glass and stay visible.
  • A spray bottle + a small jug of water — the spray bottle is the daily watering tool (and brilliant fine-motor practice).
  • 2 labels: a ☀️ SUNNY card and a 🌑 DARK card.
  • A "ruler": a small ruler, or a stack of identical LEGO/blocks to measure sprout height in "blocks."
  • The Grow Journal + crayons.
  • For the finale: a small plant pot + potting soil so Kai's favorite sprout can keep growing.
  • Optional: extra beans/lentils for the "seed sort" companion day; a magnifier for the garden walk.

Safety check

Dried beans are a choke hazard — keep them adult-handled, no mouths, and count them back in afterward. Wash hands after handling seeds and soil. Skip this project's bean-eating jokes.

Setup, the night before Day 1

  1. Soak 4–6 beans in a bowl of water overnight (soaked beans sprout days faster).
  2. Make the ☀️ SUNNY and 🌑 DARK labels.
  3. Scout your two spots: a bright windowsill, and a fully dark cupboard or drawer.
  4. Print the Grow Journal; set out crayons and the block "ruler."

Day by day, in detail

Day 1 · ★★☆

Plant the fair test

Goal: set up two identical cups and plant the idea of changing one thing on purpose.

Do

  1. Together, line each cup with a damp crumpled paper towel.
  2. Tuck 2 soaked beans into each cup, pressed against the glass so they show.
  3. Spray both the same amount. Stress the sameness: "These two are TWINS."
  4. Label them, then place one on the windowsill, one in the dark cupboard.
  5. Get his Day-1 prediction and mark it in the journal.

Ask

These two cups are exactly the same — except one gets sunshine and one stays dark. Which do you think will grow more? Let's guess!

What do you think a seed needs to wake up and grow?

Watch for

  • He notices the cups are "the same except…" — the seed of fair-test thinking.
  • He makes a confident prediction (either answer is great).
  • He may think the dark one grows (or that nothing will). Don't correct — the wait will show him.

Done when

Both cups are planted, labeled, placed, and his guess is recorded.

Every day · ~10 min

The check-in ritual

Goal: build the patience to notice slow change, plus daily counting.

Do

Same time each day (after breakfast works well). Bring the dark cup to the light just to look, spray both equally, then return the dark one. Kai draws what he sees in each cup and you write the date.

Ask

What's different from yesterday? Anything new — a crack, a root, a sprout?

How many days has it been now? Let's count on the calendar.

What do you think we'll see tomorrow?

Watch for

  • He spots small changes (a swelling bean, a tiny root) — close observation.
  • He's eager to check — anticipation is curiosity.
  • If the daily wait frustrates him, keep it to 2 minutes and lean on the companion activities for hands-on energy.

Done when

Both cups are sprayed and at least one observation is drawn/dated.

While we wait · optional 30 min

Companion: Seed sort & count

Goal: reuse Project 1's sorting + counting; reinforce "a seed holds a whole plant."

Do

Pour out a mix of dried beans, lentils, peas, sunflower seeds. Kai sorts by type into piles and counts each. Talk about how each tiny seed has a plant "sleeping" inside.

Ask

Can you put all the same ones together? How many big beans?

All of these are sleeping seeds. What do you think is inside?

Watch for

  • Accurate sorting by one feature (size/color/shape).
  • Counting hiccups are fine — model the correct count.
While we wait · optional 30 min

Companion: Plant-parts garden walk

Goal: connect the cup experiment to real, living plants.

Do

Walk outside (or visit houseplants) hunting for roots, stems, leaves, flowers. Use a magnifier. Touch, smell, compare. Tie it back: "Our beans are growing a root and a stem too!"

Ask

Where do you think this plant drinks its water?

Can you find a plant's leaves? Its stem? What about roots?

Watch for

  • He names plant parts and links them to the cups.
  • He notices plants are everywhere once he's looking.
Sprout day · ★★★

The big reveal & compare

Goal: read the result of a fair test, and measure.

Do

When sprouts appear (usually day 5–10), line both cups side by side. The sunny one is typically greener and sturdier; the dark one is often pale, leggy, and stretched. Measure each sprout in "blocks" and compare to his Day-1 guess.

Ask

Look — they're different! What do you notice about each one?

Why do you think the sunny one looks like that? Was that your guess?

How many blocks tall is the tallest sprout?

Watch for

  • He describes the difference in his own words ("that one's yellow and floppy!").
  • He connects the result to the one thing that changed (light).
  • If both grew similarly (it happens — beans are tough), that's still a real result; talk about what you'd change next time.

Done when

Both sprouts are compared and measured; result marked against his guess.

Finale · ★★★ · Kai leads

Kai's own seed

Goal: ownership, responsibility, and a living keepsake.

Do

Kai picks his favorite sprout to "adopt," plants it in the soil pot, and decides where it lives. He's in charge of watering it from now on — a gentle first responsibility.

Ask

You're the gardener now — what does your plant need to stay happy?

Where should it live? How often will you give it a drink?

Watch for

  • He recalls "plants need light and water" from the experiment.
  • Pride and care for "his" plant.

Done when

The sprout is potted and Kai has a watering plan. Keep it going past the project!

What to observe (is it working?)

  • Green flags: he checks the cups eagerly; he notices tiny daily changes; he remembers "we changed the light"; he uses words like seed, root, sprout, grow; he predicts what comes next.
  • Gently adjust: if the two-week wait drags, lean harder on companion days; if he forgets the cups, make the check-in part of an existing routine (after breakfast).
  • Don't worry about: a guess that was "wrong," a cup that grows oddly, not understanding photosynthesis, or losing interest some days. Patience itself is the skill being built.

FAQ & troubleshooting

Nothing is sprouting — did we fail?

Give it time: soaked beans usually show a root in 3–5 days, a sprout in 5–10. If after ~10 days there's nothing, the beans may be old or too dry. Check the paper towel is still damp (not soaked, not bone dry), keep it warm (room temp, not a cold windowsill), and try fresh beans. A "failure" is a great science moment: "Hmm, what could we change to help them grow?"

The wait is too long — he loses interest between check-ins.

This is the hardest part of the project, and learning to wait IS a skill. Keep daily check-ins to 2 minutes, make them a fixed routine (right after breakfast), and fill the in-between days with the hands-on companion activities (seed sort, garden walk). Beans are among the fastest sprouters, which is why we chose them.

Both cups grew about the same. Isn't the experiment ruined?

Not at all — that's a real result, and beans are hardy enough to sprout even in low light at first. Talk about it honestly: "They look similar! I wonder what would happen if we waited longer, or if one had no water at all?" An unexpected result is a perfect launchpad for the next experiment, not a failure.

Can we use a different "one thing" to change?

Yes — the fair-test idea is the point, not the specific variable. Easy swaps: water vs no water (both in light), warm spot vs fridge, or lots of water vs a drop. Just keep everything else identical and change only one thing. Ask me and I'll write you a variant.

He wants to dig the beans up every day.

Common! The clear-cup method helps — he can see the roots without digging. If he keeps disturbing them, plant a "sacrifice" bean he's allowed to pull up and inspect, while the experiment cups stay untouched. Curiosity about the underground is a good instinct to honor.

Do I need soil, or is the paper-towel method really okay?

Paper towel in a clear cup is actually better for this age — Kai can watch the roots and sprout appear, which soil hides. Save the soil for the finale, when he pots his keeper to grow on long-term.

What if mold grows?

A little can happen if it's too wet. Keep the towel damp, not soggy, and give some airflow (don't seal the cup). If you see fuzzy mold, start fresh with a clean cup and less water. You can even make it a mini-lesson: "Too much water wasn't good — what should we change?"

He keeps eating-mimicking the beans / putting them near his mouth.

Stay close and keep beans counted. Dried beans are a choke hazard and raw beans aren't for eating. If mouthing is a strong urge, you handle the beans and let him do the spraying, labeling, and drawing instead.

He's bored — can we speed this up?

Add a second, faster experiment in parallel: cress or grass seed on a damp sponge sprouts in 2–3 days for quick gratification, while the beans run the "real" fair test. Two timelines keep momentum.
