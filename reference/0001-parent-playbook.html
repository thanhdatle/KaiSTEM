The Parent Playbook

The one page to re-read before any session. The method, the magic words, the glossary, and how the journal works. Print it. Tape it inside a cupboard.

For the grown-up· Kai, age 3.5· ~3 min read

The whole method, in five moves

Every project runs on a little five-move loop. You are not delivering facts. You are narrating curiosity so Kai borrows the habit. Keep your own voice playful and a half-step behind his. The course has two tracks, each with its own loop — same spirit, tuned to how that kind of thinking grows.

🔬 Science loop

  • 👀Notice
    look closely
  • 💭Wonder
    ask "what if?"
  • 🤔Predict
    guess out loud
  • 🧪Test
    try it!
  • 💬Talk

📖 Words loop

    hear the sounds

The one rule that matters most

A guess that turns out "wrong," cheerfully tested, beats a right answer recited. Never correct a prediction — test it. Surprise is the prize.1

The magic words (your question bank)

Open-ended questions are the single highest-leverage thing you do. They have no "right" answer, so Kai keeps talking and thinking instead of fishing for approval.2 Aim for one good question, then wait — count to five in your head before rescuing the silence.

MoveSay this
Notice"What do you see? What does it feel like? Tell me about it."
Wonder"I wonder what would happen if…"   "Hmm, why do you think that is?"
Predict"What do you think will happen? Let's guess first."
Test"Should we try it and find out? You do it!"
Talk"What happened? Was that what you thought? What should we try next?"

Model wonder out loud

I wonder why this rock is so heavy but that leaf floats away…

When you wonder aloud, you show Kai that asking questions is what grown-ups do too — curiosity is a lifelong habit, not a school task.2

The Words magic words (for the 📖 track)

The Words track has its own loop, so it has its own question bank. The golden rule is different from science in one way: never correct a rhyme — just replay a good one and play on. A child who says "zat" rhymes with "cat" is hearing the pattern, which is the whole win.5

MoveSay this
Listen"Hear it? Cat… hat — they sound the same at the end!"
Wonder"I wonder what rhymes with your name…"
Play"What rhymes with frog? Real or silly — you pick!"
Make"…how I wonder what you ___?" (then wait and smile)
Share"Say me your rhyme — I'll write it down and read it back!"

How the journal works (both tracks)

Each project comes with a printable journal page. The journal is where learning becomes a keepsake — and quietly builds early literacy, counting, and memory. Science projects log a guess and a result; Words projects log the rhyme pairs Kai hears and invents — but the ritual is the same.

  • Before testing: Kai marks his prediction (a circle, a sticker, a scribble — drawing ability is not the point).
  • After testing: Kai marks what actually happened, right next to his guess.
  • You scribe: write down Kai's exact words underneath. Quote him. ("Kai said: 'the duck is swimming!'")
  • Date it and keep them in a folder. Re-reading old pages is its own lesson.

Glossary — words we use the same way every time

Use these words with Kai consistently so they stick. Keep the kid-definition simple; the grown-up note tells you what's really going on.

WordHow to say it to KaiWhat it really means
Predict / Guess"What do you think will happen?"A hypothesis — a guess made before testing.
Test"Let's try it and see!"An experiment — doing it to find out.
Observe / Notice"Look closely — what do you see?"Using the senses to gather evidence.
Sort"Put the same ones together."Classifying by an attribute — early math + logic.
Float / Sink"It stays up / it goes down."Buoyancy (we don't say that word yet!).
Same / Different"Are these the same or different?"Comparing — the root of all classification.
Fair test"Let's change just ONE thing and keep the rest the same."A controlled experiment — only one variable changes.
Over time"Let's check on it every day and see what changed."Longitudinal observation — noticing slow change.
Mix"What happens when we put these together?"Combining materials; sometimes makes something new.
Reaction"Whoa — it's fizzing/changing!"A chemical change (e.g. acid + base → gas).
Measure"How many? How tall? How long?"Putting a number on something — early math.
Classify / Group"Let's put the same kinds together — by color, by legs…"Sorting by attributes; can re-group by a new rule.
Observe closely"Use the magnifier — what tiny things do you see?"Detailed observation, often with a tool.
Shadow"A shadow happens when something blocks the light."Absence of light where an object blocks a light source.
Light source"What's making the light — the sun? the torch?"The thing giving off light (sun, lamp, flashlight).
Rhyme 📖"Words that sound the same at the end — cat, hat, bat."Phonological awareness: hearing shared ending sounds.
Sounds the same / different 📖"Do these sound the same at the end?"Discriminating sounds in speech — the root of rhyming.
Silly / made-up word 📖"Make up a NOT-real word that rhymes!"Generating a rhyme proves he hears the pattern (nonsense counts).
Make / Share a rhyme 📖"Say your own — I'll write it and read it back."Producing language + seeing speech become print (early literacy).
Beat / Syllable 📖"Words have beats — clap them! el-e-phant = 👏👏👏."Syllable: a chunk of a word. Clapping segments the word audibly.
Clap it out 📖"Say it robot-slow and clap each part."Syllable segmentation — the core word-beats move (an ear skill, not letters).
Short / Long word 📖"Few beats or lots of beats?"Comparing syllable counts; "more vs fewer" matters more than the exact number.

Running a 30-minute session without it going sideways

  • Prep the night before. Materials in a tray, ready. Scrambling = lost attention.
  • Start with a hook, not instructions. Hand him the thing. Let him touch it first.
  • Follow his lead. If he invents a better question, abandon your plan and chase his.
  • 10–20 min is plenty. "30 minutes" includes setup, mess, and snack-derail. Stop while it's still fun.
  • End on the journal. The closing ritual signals "we did real science today."

Make a session last ~30 minutes (when he finishes fast)

Curious kids often blast through the "main" task in 10 minutes. That's not a problem — it means he's ready for more reps and more depth, not a longer to-do list. Build each session in three layers:

  • Warm-up (~5 min): a hook — a question, a quick game, "remember last time?"
  • Main (~15 min): the core activity, run as several rounds, not one. Repetition is where learning sticks at this age.
  • Extend / stretch (~10 min): a harder twist, a Kai-led variation, a drawing, or "what if we change one thing?" Each detailed lesson lists Stretch it further options.

And always honor the override rule: if he's done at 12 minutes and happy, stop. A short, joyful session beats a stretched-out, fussy one.

Why this works (the evidence)

Preschoolers are genuinely capable scientists — they ask questions, predict, and reason about cause and effect already.3 Early, joyful, hands-on STEM builds a lasting "STEM identity" (Kai seeing himself as someone who figures things out),4 and early math/sorting skill is one of the strongest predictors of later school success.1 The same is true for language: rhyme and sound-play (phonological awareness) are among the strongest early predictors of how easily a child later learns to read — and they develop through play, not drill.5
