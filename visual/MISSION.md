# Mission: Visual track — see it, then turn it

> One of three tracks in KaiSTEM. The north star, the method, and the guardrails are **shared** and
> live in **[[HUB]]** (`../HUB.md`). This file states only what the **Visual** track grows and why.

## The one-line goal

Build **spatial thinking** — the ability to turn, flip, and rebuild shapes in the mind — through
shapes Kai can hold in his hands.

## Why this track exists (and why we trust it)

Of everything in this workspace, **spatial/rotation play is the best-evidenced intervention.** The
Uttal et al. 2013 meta-analysis (217 studies) found spatial training produces a durable, medium-sized
gain (**g = 0.47**) that **transfers** to untrained tasks, with **no age barrier** — it works at 3.5.
Spatial skill in early childhood predicts later maths and STEM achievement, so this is high-leverage
play, not a party trick.

This track grew out of the family's **Shichida** sessions. We kept the part that works and dropped the
part that doesn't — honestly labelled:

- ✅ **Kept:** hands-on spatial/rotation play, and *warm, focused parent–child time*. Across this whole
  literature the active ingredient is **the quality of the adult–child interaction**, not the branded
  product or content. Shichida partly worked because it sat a delighted parent and child face-to-face.
- ❌ **Dropped (neuromyth / no evidence):** the "right-brain / left-brain" mechanism is a
  **debunked neuromyth**; the brain does not have a whole-hemisphere "dominance" phenotype.

## The Visual loop

Same spirit as every track — *guess/try first, then find out, then talk* — tuned to spatial thinking:

**👀 Look → 💡 Wonder → 🔄 Turn → 🧱 Make → 💬 Talk**

- **👀 Look** — really see the shape: its corners, its sides, which way it points.
- **💡 Wonder** — "What happens if we turn it? Is it still the same shape?"
- **🔄 Turn** — rotate it, flip it, and compare — the core mental move.
- **🧱 Make** — rebuild it, or build its match, with hands and materials.
- **💬 Talk** — name what happened with **spatial words** (turn, flip, corner, edge, match).
  Parents' *spatial talk* is a measured lever on children's spatial thinking — so narrate out loud.

## Progression ladder

- **V1 · Turn It (★☆☆)** — a shape **stays itself when you turn it**, but becomes a **different shape
  when you flip it**. The distinction beneath puzzles, tangrams, and one day the letters *b* / *d*.
- *Next (planned):* repeating **patterns** (pre-K patterning uniquely predicts later maths), then
  symmetry and simple 2D→3D building. Each lesson ships on-screen **and** print-ready.

## Kai-interest hooks

Lean on what already lights him up: 🔢 shapes & puzzles, 💧 hands-on mess-and-make, and the delight
of a "trick" (turning a shape and it's *still the same!*). Keep it a game he wins, not a test.

## Track-specific guardrails ("Out of scope")

Per the [evidence card](reference/0002-visual-thinking-evidence.html), we **do not** ship:

- **No fast flash cards** — the one rigorous RCT of fast-flash baby-reading was null on 13 of 14
  measures. Speed is not the ingredient.
- **No ESP / intuition games** — no evidence whatsoever.
- **No linking-memory mnemonics before ~age 5–6** — interactive-imagery memory tricks were only
  studied from age 5–6; they don't land at 3.5.
- **No AI-generated mental-rotation cards** — geometric exactness *is* the task; a near-miss shape
  teaches the wrong thing. (AI imagery is fine for pattern sets, vocabulary, and symmetry art.)
