KaiSTEM · Reference 02

What's real, what isn't

An honest audit of “right-brain” education — and the visual skills that are worth thirty minutes of your day.

Written for the parent · Every claim linked to its source · Print it and keep it

Read this first

You told me the Shichida method helped Kai, and I believe you. This card is not here to tell you that you were fooled. It is here to separate the parts that work from the story told about why they work — because once you can see the difference, you can spend your thirty minutes on the parts that pay.

The short version: the activities are a mixed bag, and you are the active ingredient.

The claim at the centre of it all

“Right-brain education” rests on the idea that people have a dominant hemisphere — that some children are right-brained (creative, visual, intuitive), that schooling neglects them, and that a programme can wake that hemisphere up.

This is the most widely believed neuromyth in education. When Dekker and colleagues surveyed teachers, roughly 90% endorsed it (Dekker et al., 2012). When Nielsen and colleagues actually looked — resting-state fMRI across 1,011 people and 7,266 brain regions — they found that lateralization is a property of specific networks, not of persons. There is no whole-brain “left-brained” or “right-brained” type (Nielsen et al., 2013).

Be precise, though — don't over-debunk

Hemispheric specialization is real. Language is strongly left-lateralized in most right-handers; spatial attention and face processing lean right. What does not exist is the whole-person “dominance” type, and no activity trains one hemisphere in isolation — ordinary cognition recruits both (Lindell & Kidd, 2011). Anyone who tells you the myth means “the hemispheres are identical” is also wrong.

Claim by claim

The claimWhat the evidence saysVerdict
Fast flash cards bypass conscious processing and train the right brain A randomised trial of a best-selling flash-card and DVD programme — 117 infants, seven months of daily use — found no difference on 13 of 14 measures. The one difference: parents believed their babies had learned to read (Neuman et al., 2014). The US FTC charged a similar “baby can read” product with deceptive advertising. ❌ The speed does nothing
Photographic memory can be preserved or installed by training Eidetic imagery is real but uncommon (~2–10% of children), fades by adolescence, and is reconstructive — eidetic children omit and even invent details, and recall no more than their peers. True photographic memory is essentially unattested in adults (Haber; overview). ❌ Not a thing to install
Linking memory — chain pictures into a silly story, then recall the sequence Elaborative imagery strategies are late-developing. Preschoolers show “mediation deficiency”: they do not benefit even when explicitly taught. The best training study on interactive imagery begins at ages 5–6 (Miller et al., 2015). Method-of-loci studies skew to ages 9–17. ⏳ Right idea, wrong age — revisit at 5–6
ESP / intuition games — guess the hidden colour, sense the card No scientific evidence, at all. Tellingly, even enthusiastic Shichida parent-bloggers concede that any benefit comes from bonding and a rich environment rather than from extrasensory perception. ❌ No
Dot cards build instant number sense that boosts maths The correlation between approximate number sense and maths is real and replicated (Libertus et al., 2011). But the training→maths transfer claim collapsed: the original authors failed to replicate it across four experiments (Szkudlarek, Park & Brannon, 2021). ⚠️ Fun — but expect no maths gain
Memory-grid games expand working memory You reliably improve at the trained task. Far transfer to intelligence, reading or arithmetic does not survive comparison against active controls (Melby-Lervåg, Redick & Hulme, 2016; Gire et al., 2023). ⚠️ A good game, not brain training
Puzzles, tangrams, rotating shapes Meta-analysis of 217 studies: spatial training works, average effect g = 0.47, gains are durable and transfer to untrained tasks, and age is no barrier (Uttal et al., 2013). In under-8s the effect is larger still (Yang et al., 2020). Puzzle play at ages 2–4 predicts later mental-transformation skill (Levine et al., 2012). ✅ Strongly supported
Pattern play — copy, extend, and abstract a repeating pattern Repeating-pattern skill at the end of pre-K uniquely predicts maths achievement years later, controlling for other maths, reading and cognitive measures (Rittle-Johnson et al., 2020). ✅ Supported

And the finding that reframes everything

Across this whole literature one variable keeps doing the work: the quality of the adult–child interaction. Hold the material constant and change only how the adult talks — open questions, contingent responses, rich language — and you still get moderate gains (Prime et al., 2023, g = 0.46). In the Baby Einstein trial, the words infants learned came from parents teaching during ordinary daily life, not from the product (DeLoache et al., 2010).

And parents who narrate space — turn, flip, corner, edge, under, between — have children with measurably better spatial thinking (Pruden, Levine & Huttenlocher, 2011).

So Shichida probably did help Kai. Not because it woke a hemisphere, but because it sat a delighted parent opposite a delighted child for thirty focused minutes a day, naming things. That is a real mechanism. It is also one you already own — and can now point at whatever you like.

What we keep, and why

The redesign, in one table

Keep the ritual. Keep the cards. Keep the joy. Move the cognitive load onto the activity with real evidence behind it — which happens to be the one Kai already loves.

Shichida activityWhat we do with it
Flash cards, fastKeep the cards. Slow them right down. Ask “what is it?”, “what's it for?”, “which is your favourite?” The cards become a vehicle for talk — which is what was working all along.
Linking memory / story chainsKeep telling the silly stories, as stories. Drop the recall test. Revisit as a memory technique when Kai is 5–6.
Mandala / photographic memoryReframe as symmetry play: fold it, match it, complete the missing half. Real spatial content, no mystical claim.
Puzzles, tangrams, rotationPromote to the centre of the track. This is Lesson 1: Turn It.
ESP / intuition gamesDrop. If Kai loves the hide-and-guess format, keep the format as a guessing game — that is prediction, which the Science track already trains.
Dot cardsKeep as playful counting. Enjoy it. Expect no maths transfer.

How to read a claim like this yourself

You will meet more programmes like this one. Three questions cut through most of them:

  1. Is there a randomised trial with an active control? Comparing a programme against doing nothing tells you almost nothing — attention alone helps.
  2. Does the gain transfer? Getting better at the trained game is expected and nearly meaningless. Getting better at an untrained task is the entire point.
  3. Does the mechanism story do any work? If “right brain” were swapped for “left brain” throughout the brochure, would a single prediction change? If not, the mechanism is decoration.

Where to test this against other parents

Wisdom comes from arguing with people who have actually done it. Three places worth your time: r/ScienceBasedParenting, whose culture demands citations and pushes back hard on marketing claims; DREME Family Math (Stanford), which gives away the research-based spatial and number activities that programmes like Shichida sell; and Erikson's Early Math Collaborative, the best source of concrete activities.
